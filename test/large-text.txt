## Polynomial Terminology
A **polynomial** `$$p(x)$$` over a (commutative unital) ring `$$R$$` of degree `$$n$$` (for any `$$n \in \mathbb{N}$$`) is a function `$$p: R \to R$$` given by:
`$$p(x) = \sum_{i=0}^{n} a_i x^i = a_0 + a_1 x + a_2 x^2 + \dots + a_n x^n$$` for all `$$x \in R$$`, where `$$a_0, a_1, \dots, a_n \in R$$` with `$$a_n \neq 0$$` 【5】【12】【20】【29】【39】【50】.
- The terms `$$a_0, a_1, \dots, a_n$$` are called **coefficients** 【17】【25】【34】【44】【55】.
- `$$a_n$$` is the **leading coefficient** of `$$p(x)$$` 【17】【25】【34】【44】【55】.
- `$$a_0$$` is the **constant term** of `$$p(x)$$` 【17】【25】【34】【44】【55】.
- A polynomial `$$p(x)$$` is a **monic polynomial** if its leading coefficient is `$$1$$` 【26】【35】【45】【56】.
- The **degree** of a polynomial `$$p(x)$$`, written `$$\deg(p(x))$$` or `$$\deg(p)$$`, is the largest power of `$$x$$` in `$$p(x)$$`. In the general form, `$$\deg(p(x)) = n$$` 【36】【46】【57】.
- The **zero polynomial** is the function `$$z: R \to R$$` given by `$$z(x) = 0$$` for all `$$x \in R$$` (where `$$0$$` is the additive identity in `$$R$$`) 【47】【58】. The degree of the zero polynomial is undefined, but for practical purposes, it is treated as `$$-\infty$$` 【59】.

## Ring of Polynomials
- The set of all polynomials over a ring `$$R$$` is denoted `$$R[x]$$` 【62】【65】【69】.
- **Fact**: `$$R[x]$$` is itself a (commutative unital) ring under the usual operations of addition and multiplication 【66】【70】.

## Units in `$$R[x]$$`

- **Notation**: The **unit group** of a polynomial ring `$$R[x]$$` is the set of all units in `$$R[x]$$`, denoted by `$$R[x]^*$$` 【94】【98】【103】【109】【117】【126】【136】.
- **Fact**: If `$$F$$` is a field, then `$$F[x]^* = F^*$$` 【99】【104】【110】【118】【127】【137】.

## Divisibility and Irreducibles in `$$R[x]$$`
- **Definition**: Given two polynomials `$$f(x), g(x) \in R[x]$$` for some ring `$$R$$`, `$$f(x)$$` **divides** `$$g(x)$$`, written `$$f(x) | g(x)$$`, if `$$g(x) = f(x)q(x)$$` for some polynomial `$$q(x) \in R[x]$$` 【146】【149】【155】.
- **Definition**: An **irreducible polynomial** in `$$R[x]$$` is any `$$p(x) \in R[x]$$` such that:
    - `$$p(x) \neq 0$$` and `$$p(x) \notin R[x]^*$$`, and
    - whenever `$$p(x) = f(x)g(x)$$` for polynomials `$$f(x), g(x) \in R[x]$$`, we must have `$$f(x) \in R[x]^*$$` or `$$g(x) \in R[x]^*$$` 【150】【151】【152】【156】【157】【158】.
    - If `$$F$$` is a field, `$$p(x) \in F[x]$$` is irreducible if and only if whenever `$$p(x) = f(x)g(x)$$` with `$$\deg(f(x)) \le \deg(g(x))$$`, we must have `$$\deg(f(x)) = 0$$` and `$$\deg(g(x)) > 0$$` 【159】.
- **Theorem (Unique Factorisation in `$$F[x]$$)**: If `$$F$$` is a field, then every polynomial `$$f(x) \in F[x]$$` with degree greater than `$$0$$` has a unique factorisation into irreducible polynomials, up to unit factors. That is, `$$f(x)$$` can be written uniquely in the form:
    `$$f(x) = c (p_1(x))^{\alpha_1} (p_2(x))^{\alpha_2} \dots (p_k(x))^{\alpha_k}$$`
    where `$$c \in F^*$$`, each `$$p_1(x), \dots, p_k(x)$$` is a different irreducible monic polynomial in `$$F[x]$$`, and `$$\alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_k \in \mathbb{Z}^+$$` for some `$$k \in \mathbb{Z}^+$$` 【162】【163】【164】【167】【168】【169】.

## Division Theorem in `$$F[x]$$`
- **Theorem (Division Theorem)**: For any field `$$F$$` and polynomials `$$f(x), g(x) \in F[x]$$` with `$$g(x) \neq 0$$`, there exist unique polynomials `$$q(x), r(x) \in F[x]$$` such that both:
    `$$f(x) = q(x)g(x) + r(x)$$` and `$$\deg(r(x)) < \deg(g(x))$$` 【173】【174】【177】【178】【182】【183】【188】【189】.
- `$$q(x)$$` is called the **quotient** and `$$r(x)$$` the **remainder** when `$$f(x)$$` is divided by `$$g(x)$$` 【179】【184】【190】【197】.
- The zero polynomial can be a remainder, as its degree is `$$-\infty$$` 【185】【191】【198】.
- **Fact**: The Division Theorem is also true over `$$R[x]$$` for any ring `$$R$$`, as long as the leading coefficient of `$$g(x)$$` is a unit of `$$R$$` 【192】【199】.

## Greatest Common Divisors in `$$F[x]$$`
- **Definition**: For any field `$$F$$`, a **greatest common divisor (GCD)** of two polynomials `$$f(x), g(x) \in F[x]$$` is any polynomial `$$d(x) \in F[x]$$` such that:
    - both `$$d(x) | f(x)$$` and `$$d(x) | g(x)$$`, and
    - for all `$$c(x) \in F[x]$$`, if `$$c(x) | f(x)$$` and `$$c(x) | g(x)$$`, then `$$c(x) | d(x)$$` 【220】【221】【222】【225】【226】【227】【231】【232】【233】【238】【239】【240】.
- There can be more than one GCD for a pair of polynomials. For example, in `$$\mathbb{Z}_5[x]$$`, the GCDs of `$$f(x) = 2x^2 + x$$` and `$$g(x) = x^2 + 4x + 3$$` are `$$2x + 1, x + 3, 4x + 2$$`, and `$$3x + 4$$` 【228】【234】【241】.
- **Notation**: The **standard greatest common divisor** of two polynomials `$$f(x), g(x) \in F[x]$$`, denoted `$$\gcd(f(x), g(x))$$`, is the monic GCD of `$$f(x)$$` and `$$g(x)$$` (i.e., the GCD whose leading coefficient is `$$1$$`) 【243】.
    - Example: In `$$\mathbb{Z}_5[x]$$`, `$$\gcd(2x^2 + x, x^2 + 4x + 3) = x + 3$$` 【244】.

## Euclidean Algorithm in `$$F[x]$$`
- **Algorithm (Euclidean algorithm)**: Given any field `$$F$$` and polynomials `$$f(x), g(x) \in F[x]$$`, repeatedly apply the Division Theorem. First to `$$f(x)$$` and `$$g(x)$$`, then to `$$g(x)$$` and the previous remainder `$$r_0(x)$$`, then to `$$r_0(x)$$` and `$$r_1(x)$$`, and so on, until the algorithm terminates when `$$r_{n+1}(x) = 0$$`. The previous remainder `$$r_n(x)$$` is a GCD of `$$f(x)$$` and `$$g(x)$$` 【247】【250】【254】【258】【264】.
- When working over polynomial rings, any unit can be factored out from a remainder before applying the Division Theorem to it 【251】【255】【259】【265】.
- **Theorem (Bézout’s identity)**: Given any field `$$F$$` and polynomials `$$f(x), g(x) \in F[x]$$`, there exist polynomials `$$p(x), q(x) \in F[x]$$` such that:
    `$$\gcd(f(x), g(x)) = f(x)p(x) + g(x)q(x)$$` 【260】【261】【266】【267】.
    These polynomials `$$p(x)$$` and `$$q(x)$$` can be found by reversing the Euclidean algorithm or using the extended Euclidean algorithm table 【268】.

## Polynomial Congruences

## The Set of Polynomials Modulo `$$m(x)$$`

## Solving Linear Congruences
To solve the general linear congruence `$$f(x)a(x) \equiv g(x) \pmod{m(x)}$$` in `$$F[x]$$`:
1.  Find `$$d(x) = \gcd(f(x), m(x))$$`. If `$$d(x) \nmid g(x)$$`, there is no solution 【405】【411】【420】.
2.  Find polynomials `$$p(x), q(x) \in F[x]$$` such that `$$d(x) = f(x)p(x) + m(x)q(x)$$` (e.g., via the Euclidean algorithm and working backwards) 【412】【413】【414】【421】【422】【423】.
3.  The general solution is then given by `$$a(x) \equiv \frac{g(x)}{d(x)} p(x) \pmod{\frac{m(x)}{d(x)}}$$` 【424】【425】【426】【427】.

## Chinese Remainder Theorem for Polynomials
- **Theorem (Chinese Remainder Theorem)**: Suppose that `$$f(x), g_i(x), m_i(x) \in F[x]$$`, and that for the system of congruences:
    `$$f(x) \equiv g_1(x) \pmod{m_1(x)}$$`
    `$$f(x) \equiv g_2(x) \pmod{m_2(x)}$$`
    `$$\dots$$`
    `$$f(x) \equiv g_t(x) \pmod{m_t(x)}$$`
    all the moduli are pairwise coprime (i.e., `$$\gcd(m_i(x), m_j(x)) = 1$$` for all `$$i \neq j$$`). Then the system of congruences has a solution, and the solution is unique modulo `$$m_1(x)m_2(x)\dots m_t(x)$$` 【441】【442】【443】【444】【445】【448】【449】【450】【451】【452】【456】【457】【458】【459】【460】.
- To solve such a system, one takes the same approach as in the integer case: solve the first congruence, substitute the result into the second, and so on 【462】.

## Remainder and Factor Theorems
- **Theorem (Remainder Theorem)**: For any ring `$$R$$`, polynomial `$$f(x) \in R[x]$$`, and `$$a \in R$$`, the remainder when `$$f(x)$$` is divided by `$$x - a$$` is `$$f(a)$$` 【479】【482】【486】【491】【497】【504】.

## Irreducible Polynomials over `$$\mathbb{Q}$$` and `$$\mathbb{Z}$$`
- **Theorem**: A polynomial `$$f(x)$$` with integer coefficients that altogether have a GCD of `$$1$$` is irreducible in `$$\mathbb{Q}[x]$$` if and only if it is irreducible in `$$\mathbb{Z}[x]$$` 【609】【613】【618】【624】. ## Finite Fields
- **Definition**: A **finite field** is a field with a finite number of elements 【763】【766】【771】【777】【784】.
- **Definition**: The **characteristic** of a finite field `$$F$$` is the smallest `$$n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$$` such that `$$\sum_{i=1}^n 1 = 1 + 1 + \dots + 1 \text{ (n copies)} = 0$$` in `$$F$$` 【767】【768】【772】【773】【778】【779】【785】【786】.
- Consider the field `$$F = \mathbb{Z}_p[x]/\langle m(x) \rangle$$`, where `$$p$$` is a prime number and `$$\deg(m(x)) = k \in \mathbb{Z}^+$$` with `$$m(x)$$` irreducible in `$$\mathbb{Z}_p[x]$$`. Then:
    - `$$F$$` has characteristic `$$p$$` 【781】【788】.
    - `$$F$$` has `$$p^k$$` elements (sometimes called order `$$p^k$$`) 【789】.
- **Theorem**: The characteristic of any finite field is a prime number 【792】【796】【801】.
- **Theorem**: The size (or order) of any finite field is `$$p^k$$` for some prime number `$$p$$` and positive integer `$$k$$` 【798】【803】.
- **Theorem**: For every prime number `$$p$$` and positive integer `$$k$$`, there exists a finite field of size (or order) `$$p^k$$` 【811】【815】【820】【826】【833】【841】.
- **Definition (loose)**: Two rings `$$R$$` and `$$S$$` are **isomorphic**, written `$$R \cong S$$`, if each element in `$$R$$` can be mapped to a different element in `$$S$$` such that their addition and multiplication tables are identical under the mapping. Example: `$$\mathbb{Z}[x]/\langle x^2 + 1 \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}[i]$$` by mapping `$$x \mapsto i$$` 【816】【821】【827】【834】【842】.
- **Theorem**: For a given prime number `$$p$$` and positive integer `$$k$$`, there is only one finite field of size `$$p^k$$` up to isomorphism 【822】【828】【835】【843】.
- **Definition**: The finite field of size `$$p^k$$` is called the **Galois field of order `$$p^k$$`** and denoted as `$$GF(p^k)$$`, or more commonly, as `$$\mathbb{F}_{p^k}$$` (or just `$$\mathbb{F}_q$$` where `$$q = p^k$$`) 【829】【836】【844】.
- **Corollary**: `$$\mathbb{F}_{p^k} \cong \mathbb{Z}_p[x]/\langle m(x) \rangle$$` for any irreducible polynomial `$$m(x)$$` in `$$\mathbb{Z}_p[x]$$` with degree `$$k$$` 【837】【845】. These theorems allow for the complete characterization and construction of every possible finite field 【846】.

## Order and Primitive Elements in Finite Fields
- For `$$F_q = \mathbb{F}_{p^k}$$` where `$$p$$` is a prime number and `$$k \in \mathbb{Z}^+$$` (so `$$q = p^k$$`) 【865】【868】【872】【877】【883】【890】【898】【907】【917】.
- In any field `$$F$$`, every element except `$$0$$` is a unit, so `$$F^* = F - \{0\}$$` 【869】【873】【878】【884】【891】【899】【908】【918】.
- **Definition**: Given any `$$\alpha \in F_q^*$$`, the **order of `$$\alpha$$` in `$$F_q$$`**, written `$$\text{ord}_{F_q}(\alpha)$$`, is the smallest positive integer `$$t$$` such that `$$\alpha^t = 1$$` in `$$F_q$$` 【874】【879】【885】【892】【900】【909】【919】.
- **Theorem (Generalisation of Fermat’s Little Theorem)**: For any `$$\alpha \in F_q^*$$`, we have `$$\alpha^{q-1} = 1$$` in `$$F_q$$`. Equivalently, for any `$$\alpha \in F_q$$`, we have `$$\alpha^q = \alpha$$` in `$$F_q$$` 【895】【903】【912】【922】.
- **Theorem**: For any `$$\alpha \in F_q^*$$`, we have that `$$\text{ord}_{F_q}(\alpha) | q - 1$$` 【904】【913】【923】.
- **Definition**: A **primitive element** of `$$F_q$$` (or of `$$F_q^*$$`) is any element `$$\alpha \in F_q^*$$` such that `$$\text{ord}_{F_q}(\alpha) = q - 1$$` 【914】【924】.
- **Theorem**: If `$$\alpha$$` is a primitive element of `$$F_q$$`, then `$$\alpha$$` is a generator of `$$F_q^*$$`. That is, `$$\langle \alpha \rangle = \{\alpha^0, \alpha^1, \alpha^2, \dots, \alpha^{q-1}\} = F_q^*$$` 【925】.

## Finding Primitive Elements
- **Theorem**: Every finite field `$$F_q$$` has a primitive element 【928】【931】【935】【940】【946】【953】【961】【970】【980】.
- **Method to find a primitive element of `$$F_q = \mathbb{Z}_p[x]/\langle m(x) \rangle$$` (where `$$m(x)$$` is irreducible of degree `$$k$$`)**:
    1.  Choose `$$\alpha$$` to be a root of `$$m(x)$$` in `$$\mathbb{Z}_p[x]$$` (i.e., let `$$\alpha$$` be an element of `$$F_q$$` satisfying `$$m(\alpha) = 0$$`) 【943】【949】【956】【964】【973】【983】.
    2.  For each prime divisor `$$p_i$$` of `$$q - 1$$`, check `$$\alpha^{(q-1)/p_i}$$` in `$$F_q$$` 【950】【957】【965】【974】【984】.
    3.  If `$$\alpha^{(q-1)/p_i} \neq 1$$` in `$$F_q$$` for all prime divisors `$$p_i$$`, then `$$\alpha$$` is a primitive element of `$$F_q$$` 【958】【966】【975】【985】.
    4.  Otherwise, test a new element `$$\beta = \alpha + 1$$`, and so on 【967】【976】【986】.
- **Theorem**: Given `$$\alpha$$` is a primitive element of `$$F_q$$`, `$$\alpha^t$$` is a primitive element of `$$F_q$$` if and only if `$$\gcd(t, q - 1) = 1$$` 【977】【987】【998】.
- **Corollary**: Every finite field `$$F_q$$` has exactly `$$\phi(q - 1)$$` primitive elements 【988】【999】.

## Finite Fields as Vector Spaces
- **Theorem**: If `$$\alpha$$` is a primitive element of a finite field `$$F_q$$` (with `$$q = p^k$$`), then `$$F_q$$` is a vector space over `$$\mathbb{Z}_p[x]$$` with basis `$$\{1, \alpha, \alpha^2, \dots, \alpha^{k-1}\}$$` 【1060】.
- This means every element of `$$F_q$$` can be written as a unique linear combination of `$$1, \alpha, \alpha^2, \dots, \alpha^{k-1}$$` over `$$\mathbb{Z}_p[x]$$` 【1061】.

## Irreducible Factors
- From the generalization of Fermat’s little theorem, for any `$$\alpha \in F_q$$`, we have `$$\alpha^q = \alpha$$`. Thus, every element of `$$F_q$$` is a root of the polynomial `$$x^q - x$$` 【1073】【1078】.
- **Theorem**: Every irreducible polynomial in `$$\mathbb{Z}_p[x]$$` of degree `$$k$$` is a factor of `$$x^q - x$$` where `$$q = p^k$$` 【1074】【1079】.

## Minimal Polynomials
- **Definition**: Given any `$$\alpha \in F_q \cong \mathbb{Z}_p[x]/\langle m(x) \rangle$$`, the **minimal polynomial of `$$\alpha$$` in `$$\mathbb{Z}_p[x]$$`**, denoted `$$m_\alpha(x)$$`, is the monic polynomial of smallest degree such that `$$m_\alpha(\alpha) = 0$$` 【1091】.
- **Theorem**: Given any `$$\alpha \in F_q$$`, the minimal polynomial of `$$\alpha$$` in `$$\mathbb{Z}_p[x]$$` is unique 【1094】【1098】.
- **Corollary**: Given any `$$\alpha \in F_q$$` and a monic polynomial `$$f(x) \in \mathbb{Z}_p[x]/\langle m(x) \rangle$$` such that `$$f(\alpha) = 0$$`, if `$$f(x)$$` is irreducible in `$$\mathbb{Z}_p[x]$$` then it is the minimal polynomial of `$$\alpha$$` in `$$\mathbb{Z}_p[x]$$` 【1100】.

### Finding Minimal Polynomials
- **Theorem**: Suppose `$$\alpha \in F_q$$` and `$$r$$` is the smallest positive integer such that `$$\alpha^{p^r} = \alpha$$` in `$$F$$`. Then the minimal polynomial of `$$\alpha$$` is given by:
    `$$m_\alpha(x) = (x - \alpha)(x - \alpha^p)(x - \alpha^{p^2}) \dots (x - \alpha^{p^{r-1}})$$` 【1103】【1104】【1107】【1108】【1112】【1113】【1118】【1119】【1125】【1126】【1134】【1135】【1144】【1145】. This formula is often computationally intensive 【1115】【1121】【1128】【1137】.
- **Method to find the minimal polynomial of any element `$$\beta \in F_q$$` (where `$$q = p^k$$`)**:
    1.  Find a primitive element `$$\alpha$$` of `$$F_q$$` 【1131】【1140】【1150】.
    2.  Write each element `$$\beta^0, \beta^1, \beta^2, \dots, \beta^k$$` as a linear combination of the basis `$$\{1, \alpha, \alpha^2, \dots, \alpha^{k-1}\}$$` 【1141】【1151】.
    3.  Find the nontrivial linear combination of `$$\{\beta^0, \beta^1, \dots, \beta^k\}$$` that equals `$$0$$` and, as a polynomial in `$$\beta$$`, is monic and of lowest possible degree. Replacing each `$$\beta$$` with `$$x$$` then gives the minimal polynomial `$$m_\beta(x)$$` in `$$F_q$$` 【1152】.

## Cyclotomic Cosets
- **Theorem**: Suppose `$$p$$` is prime and `$$f(x) \in \mathbb{Z}_p[x]$$`. If `$$\alpha$$` is a root of `$$f(x)$$`, then `$$\alpha^p$$` is a root of `$$f(x)$$` 【1168】【1172】【1178】【1187】.
- **Corollary**: Suppose `$$\alpha \in F_q$$` and `$$r$$` is the smallest positive integer such that `$$\alpha^{p^r} = \alpha$$` in `$$F$$`. Then the minimal polynomial of `$$\alpha$$` is given by `$$m_\alpha(x) = (x - \alpha)(x - \alpha^p)(x - \alpha^{p^2}) \dots (x - \alpha^{p^{r-1}})$$` 【1174】【1175】【1180】【1181】【1189】【1190】.
- **Definition**: Let `$$q = p^k$$` for a prime `$$p$$` and `$$k \in \mathbb{Z}^+$$`, and let `$$s \in \mathbb{Z}_{q-1}$$`. The **cyclotomic coset** for `$$\mathbb{Z}_{q-1}$$` containing `$$s$$` is the set `$$K_s = \{sp^i \pmod{q-1} : i \in \mathbb{N}\} = \{s, sp, sp^2, sp^3, \dots \}$$` in `$$\mathbb{Z}_{q-1}$$` 【1183】【1184】【1192】【1193】.
- **Corollary**: If `$$\alpha$$` is primitive in `$$F_q$$`, then the minimal polynomial of `$$\alpha^s$$` for any integer `$$s$$` is also the minimal polynomial of `$$\alpha^t$$` for all `$$t \in K_s$$` 【1194】.

## Hamming Codes as Polynomials
- Hamming codes can be interpreted in terms of polynomials. For a non-standard Hamming matrix `$$H$$` and a root `$$\alpha$$` of `$$x^3 + x + 1$$` in `$$F = \mathbb{Z}_2[x]/\langle x^3 + x + 1 \rangle$$`, if `$$x = (a_0, a_1, \dots, a_6)$$` is a valid encoded message (such that `$$Hx^T = 0$$`), then `$$\alpha$$` is a root of `$$f(x) = \sum_{i=0}^6 a_i x^i$$` 【1209】【1210】【1215】【1218】【1219】【1220】【1221】【1224】【1225】【1229】【1230】【1231】【1232】【1233】【1236】【1237】【1241】【1242】【1245】.
- If a received message `$$y$$` has an error in its `$$i$$`-th digit such that `$$Hy^T$$` is the `$$i$$`-th column of `$$H$$`, then `$$(1, \alpha, \alpha^2)Hy^T = \alpha^i$$` 【1246】.

## BCH Codes
- **Bose-Chaudhuri-Hocquenghem (BCH) codes** are a special class of error-correcting codes that expand upon the polynomial interpretation of Hamming codes 【1249】【1252】【1257】【1263】.
- BCH codes are efficient, having a high **information rate** (ratio of information digits to total digits) 【1253】【1258】【1264】. For example, the Hamming code described has an information rate of `$$\frac{4}{7}$$` 【1254】【1259】.
- BCH codes involve converting a digital message to a polynomial (digits as coefficients). **Encoding** adds check digits determined by polynomial division. **Decoding** simplifies the polynomial using finite field arithmetic to detect error number and position 【1260】【1266】.
- The standard conversion method for this course is to arrange polynomial terms in increasing order of exponents of `$$x$$`, reading message coefficients from left to right 【1267】.

### Single Error-Correcting BCH Codes – Encoding
- Encodes base-`$$p$$` messages of length `$$r$$` information digits to include `$$s$$` check digits, where `$$r + s = p^k - 1$$` and `$$s = k$$` 【1270】【1273】【1277】【1282】【1288】【1295】【1304】【1317】.
- **Set-up**:
    1.  Choose an irreducible polynomial `$$f(x)$$` of degree `$$k$$` in `$$\mathbb{Z}_p[x]$$`, and set `$$F = \mathbb{Z}_p[x]/\langle f(x) \rangle$$` 【1279】【1284】【1290】【1297】【1306】【1319】.
    2.  Find a primitive element `$$\alpha$$` in `$$F$$` and its minimal polynomial `$$m_\alpha(x)$$` 【1285】【1291】【1298】【1307】【1320】.
- **Encoding (given message `$$(a_0, a_1, \dots, a_{r-1})$$)**:
    1.  Write the information polynomial `$$I(x) = x^s \sum_{i=0}^{r-1} a_i x^i$$` 【1300】【1301】【1309】【1310】【1322】【1323】.
    2.  Find the check polynomial `$$R(x) = I(x) \pmod{M(x)}$$` where `$$M(x) = m_\alpha(x)$$`. `$$R(x) = \sum_{i=0}^{s-1} c_i x^i$$` where `$$c_i$$` are the check digits 【1311】【1312】【1313】【1314】【1324】【1325】【1326】【1327】.
    3.  Write the codeword polynomial `$$C(x) = -R(x) + I(x)$$`. For `$$p=2$$`, `$$C(x) = R(x) + I(x)$$`. Transmit the coefficients of `$$C(x)$$`, which form the vector `$$(c_0, c_1, \dots, c_{s-1}, a_0, a_1, \dots, a_{r-1})$$` 【1328】.

### Single Error-Correcting BCH Codes – Decoding
- **Decoding (given received message `$$(d_0, d_1, \dots, d_{r+s-1})$$)**:
    1.  Write the received polynomial `$$D(x) = \sum_{i=0}^{r+s-1} d_i x^i$$` 【1338】【1339】【1344】【1345】【1353】【1354】【1361】【1362】【1371】【1372】.
    2.  Evaluate `$$D(\alpha)$$` in `$$F$$` (e.g., using a power table) 【1347】【1355】【1364】【1374】.
        - If `$$D(\alpha) = 0$$`, there is no error 【1356】【1365】【1375】.
        - Otherwise, `$$D(\alpha) = \alpha^i$$` for some `$$0 \le i \le k-1$$`. Assuming at most one error, the error exists at the coefficient of `$$x^i$$` (the `$$(i+1)$$`-th digit) 【1366】【1376】.
    3.  Correct any error and extract the original message as the last `$$r$$` coefficients of the corrected polynomial, i.e., `$$(d_s, d_{s+1}, \dots, d_{r+s-1})$$` 【1377】.

### Double Error-Correcting BCH Codes – Encoding
- Encodes base-`$$p$$` messages of length `$$r$$` information digits to include `$$s$$` check digits, where `$$s$$` is the degree of the modulus polynomial and `$$r + s = p^k - 1$$` 【1396】【1399】【1403】【1408】【1414】【1421】【1428】【1438】【1449】.
- **Set-up**:

### Double Error-Correcting BCH Codes – Decoding
- **Decoding (given received message `$$(d_0, d_1, \dots, d_{r+s-1})$$)**:

## Multiple Error-Correcting BCH Codes
- This section is considered extension material and is not examinable 【1585】【1588】【1592】【1597】.
- The construction of BCH codes can be extended for multiple error correction by incorporating more minimal polynomials for powers of a primitive element whose exponents’ cyclotomic cosets are disjoint 【1594】【1599】.
- This section focuses on the binary case (`$$p=2$$`). Encoding is as expected, but decoding is more complicated 【1600】.

### Standard Binary BCH Codes
- **Definition**: The **standard binary BCH `$(2^k - 1, r)$` code** encodes binary messages of length `$$r$$` information digits to include `$$s$$` check digits, where `$$s$$` is the degree of the modulus polynomial and `$$r + s = 2^k - 1$$` 【1603】【1608】.
- For a chosen primitive element `$$\alpha$$`, the modulus polynomial is defined as:
    `$$M(x) = \text{lcm}(m_\alpha(x), m_{\alpha^2}(x), m_{\alpha^3}(x), \dots, m_{\alpha^{2t}}(x))$$`
    where `$$t$$` is any positive integer such that `$$2t < 2^k$$` 【1604】【1605】【1609】【1610】.
- **Fact**: The standard binary BCH `$(2^k - 1, r)$` code can correct up to `$$t$$` errors 【1611】.

- **Set-up (for binary multiple error-correcting BCH `$(2^k - 1, r)$` code)**:

- **Decoding (given received binary message `$$(d_0, d_1, \dots, d_{r+s-1})$$)**:
